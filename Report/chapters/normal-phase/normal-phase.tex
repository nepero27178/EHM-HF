\chapter{The normal phase}\label{chap:normal-phase}

This chapter is devoted to the MFT analysis of the ``normal phase'', which is, the one where interacting electrons do not develop an antiferromagnetic or superconducting gap, although undergoing an effective non-trivial transformation. What will be derived here, in particular the hopping renormalization effect, constitutes a peculiarity of effective MFT treatment of the EHM and will be the groundwork of following analyses. Note that this chapter is structured in such a way that what is derived here mathematically is recovered and expanded to more complex phases in next chapters.

\section{The ``normal'' phase and its symmetries}\label{sec:normal-phase-theory}

The normal phase is simply the one where no symmetry is broken, and the many-body ground state preserves the entire $\mathrm{U}(2)$ structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ehm-global-u2-symmetry-u1-su2} as well as full translational symmetry $\mathrm{T}_1^{(x)} \otimes \mathrm{T}_1^{(y)}$. In terms of spatial symmetries, we here theoretically allow for
\[
	\mathrm{C}_4 \SSB \mathrm{C}_2
\]
symmetry reduction and extract the mathematical interesting consequences. {\color{tabred} While this implies immediately inversion symmetry preservation, full $\mathrm{C}_4$ symmetry will be imposed later.}

The normal phase is essentially given by the null gap limit of both the AF and SC phases. By discussing it separately now, we highlight a MFT feature of the EHM of particular interest, namely, the insurgence of a new, self-consistent hopping parameter. From Tabs.~\ref{tab:U-wick-terms-phases} and \ref{tab:V-wick-terms-phases}, we know that the relevant Wick's contractions (RWCs) for this phase are
\[
\begin{aligned}
	&\hat H_U 
	&&\text{Hartree contraction} \\
	&\hat H_V \text{ (o.s. sector)}
	&&\text{Hartree contraction} \\
	&\hat H_V \text{ (s.s. sector)}
	&&\text{Hartree and Fock contractions} \\
\end{aligned}	
\]
We anticipate the final effects of all RWCs. As we will see in detail and is reported in Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-RWCs}, the Hartree contractions account essentially for chemical potential shifts, while the only Fock contraction for a renormalization effects on the bare bands.

\begin{table}[t]
	\centering

		\textbf{Operator} & \textbf{Sector} & \textbf{RWCs} & \textbf{Net effect} \\
		\midrule
		$\hat H_U$ && Hartree & $\mu$ shift by $-nU$ \\
		\midrule
		\multirow{3}{*}{$\hat H_V$} & o.s. & Hartree & $\mu$ shift by $nzV$ \\[0.5em]
		 & \multirow{2}{*}{s.s.} & Hartree & $\mu$ shift by $nzV$\\
		&& Fock & Bands renormalization and resymmetrization \\
		\midrule
	\end{tabular}
	\caption{Relevant Wick's contractions and net effect in the normal phase.}
	\label{tab:normal-RWCs}
\end{table}

In the normal phase, spin is a pure physical degeneracy, being the $\uparrow$ and $\downarrow$ sectors separated; moreover, $\hat H$ is $\mathbb{Z}_2$ invariant with respect to spin inversion, $\sigma \leftrightarrow \overline{\sigma}$. This implies that all physical quantities, as is for the normal phase, should not depend on the spin index. We will perform all computations leaving $\sigma$ explicit and using this consideration at the end.

\section{Hartree effects: chemical potential renormalization}\label{sec:normal-hartree-mu}

The first RMP we discuss is $\mu$, whose numerical value is renormalized by Hartree channel interactions within MFT. As is discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}, in the context of numerical simulations at fixed density, the chemical potential is determined self-consistently; thus any net effect that shifts $\mu$ is effectively ignored within (and limitedly to) the iterative algorithm. Renormalization effects become relevant when computing free energy.

\subsection{Hartree channel in the normal phase: analytic derivation}\label{subsec:normal-hartree-analytic}

Within this section we discuss analytically the effects of MFT on the Hartree channel, separating the local and non-local interactions and obtaining a set of HFPs needed to setup an interative HF algorithm.

\paragraph{Local repulsion.}

In the normal phase, the local repulsion $\hat H_U$ decomposes in Hartree channel as
\[
	\hat H_U \simeq
	U \sum_{i} \left[
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\uparrow}
		\rangle \hat n_{i\downarrow} + \hat n_{i\uparrow} \langle 
			\hat n_{i\downarrow}
		\rangle - \langle 
			\hat n_{i\downarrow}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{i\uparrow}
			\rangle
	\right]
\]
For a translationally invariant phase such as the normal phase, it holds
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-density-ansatz}
	\langle \hat n_{i\sigma} \rangle = n
\end{equation}
which gives immediately
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\hat H_U &\simeq
	nU \sum_i \left[
		 \hat n_{i\uparrow} + \hat n_{i\downarrow}
	\right]
	- U \sum_i n^2 \\
	&= nU \times \hat N - E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})}
\end{aligned}
\]
being $\hat N$ the total number operator and $ E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ the ``contraction energy'' (also known as double counting term) due to the Hartree (H) contraction of the $U$ repulsion, discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:normal-hartree-energy}. The chemical potential is corrected by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:local-hartree-mu-shift}
	\mu \to \mu - nU
\end{equation}
The non-local attraction further corrects $\mu$.

\paragraph{Non-local attraction.}

Let us break down $\hat H_V$ isolating its Hartree RWCs in both o.s. and s.s. sectors:
\begin{multline}
	\hat H_V \simeq 
	\overbrace{
		-V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
			\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \hat n_{j\sigma} + \hat n_{i\sigma} \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
			\rangle - \langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
			\rangle
		\right]
	}^{\mathrm{s.s.}} \\
	\underbrace{
		-V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
			\langle
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}} + \hat n_{i\sigma} \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
			\rangle
			- \langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
			\rangle
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{o.s.}} \, +\, 
	(\text{all the rest})
	\label{eq:normal-V-wick-decomposition}
\end{multline}
where ``all the rest'' collects all non-Hartree RWCs. Recalling the Ansatz of Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-density-ansatz}, we get
\[
	\hat H_V \simeq  \underbrace{
		-nV \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat n_{j\sigma} + \hat n_{i\sigma}
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{s.s.}}
	\underbrace{
		-nV \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}} + \hat n_{i\sigma}
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{o.s.}} 
	- E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}
	+ (\text{all the rest})
\]
with $E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ the normal state shift to energy brought by $\hat H_V$. Now, evidently both sums above are just a number operator, thus including the degeneracies introduced by the summations on NNs for a square lattice with coordination number $z=4$
\[
	\hat H_V \simeq -2nzV \times \hat N - E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} + 
	(\text{all the rest})
\]
This accounts for the final Hartree shift of $\mu$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:non-local-hartree-mu-shift}
	\mu \to \mu + 2znV
\end{equation}

Considering the net shift to $\mu$ due to both interactions by combining Eqns.~\eqref{eq:local-hartree-mu-shift}, \eqref{eq:non-local-hartree-mu-shift}, we get the final RMP anticipated in Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-RWCs},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-mu-shift}
	\tilde{\mu} \equiv \mu + n(2zV-U)
\end{equation}
This result remains valid throughout all phases discussed in this text: for the AF phase, the SDW character leaves this shift untouched, while the SC phase we will discuss is inherently translational invariant.

\subsection{Hartree contraction energy}\label{subsec:normal-hartree-energy}

The double counting terms accounting for energy shifts in the Hartree channel are, as anticipated:
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= \sum_i \langle 
		\hat n_{i\downarrow}
	\rangle \langle 
		\hat n_{i\uparrow}
	\rangle \nonumber \\
	&= L_x L_y \times n^2U \label{eq:normal-hartree-U-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
for the local repulsion and 
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= - V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
		\rangle
		+
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
		\rangle
	\right] \nonumber \\
	&= -2V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma n^2 \nonumber \\
	&= - L_x L_y \times 2zn^2 V \label{eq:normal-hartree-V-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
for the non-local attraction. In the last passage, we used Eq.~\eqref{eq:square-NN-ghost-sum}, as well as spin degeneracy. Note that, correctly, the contraction energy is an extensive quantity. This implies that we can define a contraction energy per site -- something we will do later when computing total free energy density.

\section{Fock effects: bare bands renormalization}\label{sec:normal-fock-bands-renormalization}

Up to now, no relevant changes have been made to the starting situation of the conventional degenerate Fermi gas. The scenario changes now, with the analysis of the Fock RWCs. Even if no gap opens, in the normal phase particles interact by the means of the non-local attraction $\hat H_V$, a feature that effectively redefines the bare fermionic bands.

From Wick's decomposition of $\hat H_V$, the only allowed Fock term comes from the same-spin part due to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ symmetry selection rules. Let us focus only on this term when decomposing $\hat H$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:real-space-non-local-interaction-fock}
	\hat H_V \simeq (\text{Hartree channel}) + 
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right]
\end{equation}
(note the $+$ sign in front of the displayed term). A bond-wise hopping amplitude can be defined,
\[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \equiv t - V \langle
	\hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{i\sigma}
	\rangle
\]
The effective kinetic hamiltonian is given by
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\hat H_{\tilde{t}} &= \hat H_t + V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + \hc
	\right] \\
	&= - \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma 
	\left[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right]
\end{aligned}
\]
To proceed, it is useful to move to reciprocal space.

\subsection{Fock channel in the normal phase: analytic derivation}\label{subsec:normal-fock-analytic}

In reciprocal space, the effective hopping must be transformed as well. Consider the Fourier Transform $\mathcal{F}$ given in Eq.~\eqref{eq:HV-fourier}, applied to the displayed part of Eq.~\eqref{eq:real-space-non-local-interaction-fock},
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\cos \left(
	\delta k_x
	\right)	+ \cos \left(
	\delta k_y
	\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}' \sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int1}
\end{multline}
Here, the $2$ prefactor comes from recognizing that the first two operators in square brackets in the first line generate identical contributions to the full sum (as is easy to see by performing trivial variables changes); the double counting energy shift is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-V-fock-double-counting}
	E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv \frac{V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta k_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta k_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}' \sigma}
	\rangle \langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}'\sigma} 
	\rangle
\end{equation}

Up to this point, this derivation is general and will be re-used in next chapters. Here the specificity of the physical phase settles in: if we assume the normal state to be a free degenerate Fermi gas with renormalized bands, we get
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-cc-EV}
	\langle \hat c_{\mathbf{k}_1\sigma_1}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{k}_2 \sigma_2} \rangle = \delta_{\mathbf{k}_1=\mathbf{k}_2} \delta_{\sigma_1=\sigma_2} f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\end{equation}
being $f$ the Fermi-Dirac distribution,
\[
	f(\epsilon;\beta,\mu) \equiv \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon-\mu)}+1}
\]
In other words, we are assuming a solution to exist, we call ``normal'', such that the net effect of the interactions is to generate an effective correction to bands such that the final hamiltonian is just $\hat H \simeq \hat H_{\tilde{t}} + (\text{shifts})$. It follows that Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int1} becomes
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle
			\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\langle 
			\hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} 
		\rangle - \langle 
			\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma}
		\rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\cos \left(
	2 k_x
	\right)	+ \cos \left(
	2 k_y
	\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int2}
\end{multline}
since the only contribution comes from $\mathbf{k}'=-\mathbf{k}$.

Let now $\mathbf{q}\equiv\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{q}'\equiv\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}$. A representation of this variables change is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:Kk-qq'}. Being for $\ell=x,y$
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\delta q_\ell &\equiv q_\ell - q_\ell' \\
	&= (K_\ell+k_\ell) - (K_\ell-k_\ell) \\
	&= 2k_\ell
\end{aligned}
\]
we reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int1} to
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{q}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta q_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta q_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger 
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int3}
\end{multline}

Recall now the result of the symmetry channels decomposition of Eq.~\eqref{eq:coscos-symmetries-decomposition},
\[
	\cos \left( \delta q_x \right)	+ \cos \left( \delta q_y  \right) = \frac 1 2 \sum_\gamma \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)} \varphi_{\mathbf{q}'}^{(\gamma)*}
	\qq{for $\gamma \in \lbrace s^*, p_x, p_y, d_{x^2-y^2} \rbrace$}
\]
Thanks to this handy property, we reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int3} to
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \sum_{\gamma,\sigma} \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_\mathbf{q\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)}
		u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\right] \times V
	\sum_{\mathbf{q}'\in\mathrm{BZ}}
	\varphi_{\mathbf{q}'}^{(\gamma)*}
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int4}
\end{multline}
where we defined the EV:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-uqsigma-def}
	u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} \equiv \langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
	\qquad
	u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} = u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^*
\end{equation}
Of course, the numerical value of $u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}$ is given by the Fermi-Dirac distribution; however, for the sake of reproducibility of this derivation in next chapters, we omit that for a moment. Let $u^{(\gamma)}_\sigma$ be its $\gamma$-wave component,
\[
	u^{(\gamma)}_\sigma = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)}
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
\]

Now, the bare bands $\epsilon_\mathbf{k}$ are $s^*$-wave symmetric, but in general $\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}$ can exhibit a more complex symmetry. We assume respected spatial inversion invariance, so it must be
\[
	u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} = u_{-\mathbf{q}\sigma}
\]
thus
\[
	u_\sigma^{(p_\ell)} = 0
	\qq{for}
	\ell\in\lbrace x,y \rbrace
\]
recalling the antisymmetric form factors of Tab~\ref{tab:x-wave-reciprocal-factors}. The remaining $s^*$ and $d$ symmetries are legitimate HFPs with relative self-consistency equations, reported in Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-ehm-ugamma-self-consistencies} using spin degeneracy. It is useful to anticipate here that, as will result from Sec.~\ref{subsec:normal-bands-shrinking-C2}, that the $d$-wave component will be suppressed. To prove this statement requires a little space, thus we will deal with it later on. For now we keep this component ignoring these anticipations, for reasons that will become clear in Sec.~\ref{subsec:normal-d-wave-hopping}.

The two HFPs $u_\sigma^{(s^*)}$ and $u_\sigma^{(d)}$ are in general non-zero, since we are allowing theoretically the possibility for $\mathrm{C}_4 \to \mathrm{C}_2$ SSB. Note that when imposing $\mathrm{C}_4$ invariance the $u_\sigma^{(d)}$ HFP vanishes, while in general $u_\sigma^{(s^*)}$ is non-zero. From these considerations Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int4} becomes
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} V
	\sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \sum_\sigma
	\left[
		u^{(s^*)}_\sigma
		\varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(s^*)*} + u^{(d)}_\sigma
		\varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(d)*}
	\right]
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int5}
\end{multline}
Let 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-bands-renormalization}
	\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} \equiv \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + u^{(s^*)}_\sigma V (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) + u^{(d)}_\sigma V (\cos k_x - \cos k_y)
\end{equation}
This equation defines the bands renormalization and resymmetrization anticipated in Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-RWCs}, and is actually spin-independent. The fact that both a $s^*$ and a $d$-wave component appear introduces an interesting anisotropy that is discussed in next section.

\subsection{Fock contraction energy}\label{subsec:normal-fock-energy}

Finally, let us derive the Fock contraction energy by expanding Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-double-counting}:
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= \frac{V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{ \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{q}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta q_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta q_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q} \sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{q}' \sigma}
	\rangle \langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{q}' \sigma}
	\rangle \nonumber \\
	&= L_x L_y \times \frac{V}{2} \sum_{\gamma,\sigma} \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)} u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\right] \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}'\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}'^{(\gamma)*} u_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}^*
	\right] \nonumber \\
	&= L_x L_y \times \frac{V}{2} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\big|
			u_\sigma^{(s^*)}
		\big|^2 + \big|
			u_\sigma^{(d)}
		\big|^2
	\right] \label{eq:normal-fock-V-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
This expression gives the Fock contraction energy coming from the $\hat H_V$ operator. The particularly relevant result, here, is the fact that the full spectral amplitude of $u_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}$ here is weighted and contributes to contraction energy with a $V$ linear prefactor. This suggests that a mixed-symmetry band could be energetically preferred.

\subsection{Nematicity: extending hopping to $d$-wave bands}\label{subsec:normal-d-wave-hopping}

\begin{figure}
	\centering
	\input{\GraphicsFolder/anisotropic-hopping}
	\caption{Graphic rendition of anisotropic hopping, with a $s^*$-wave component (symmetric by $\pi/$ rotations) and a $d$-wave component (antisymmetric).}
	\label{fig:anisotropic-hopping}
\end{figure}

This section serves as a zoom on the subject of the topologies of free band structures, and to justify the interest in a $\mathrm{C}_4 \to \mathrm{C}_2$ SSB apparently pretty arbitrary. {\color{tabred}[Insert: nematicity is a common feature in many materials, so on and so forth...]} From Tabs.~\ref{tab:x-wave-real-factors} and \ref{tab:x-wave-reciprocal-factors} we can inverse-Fourier transform the form factors to get the full effective hopping,
\[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} = \sum_{\gamma\in\lbrace s^*,d \rbrace} t^{(\gamma)}_\sigma \varphi_{ij}^{(\gamma)}
\]
with
\[
	t^{(s^*)}_\sigma = t - \frac{u_\sigma^{(s^*)} V}{2}
	\qquad
	t^{(d)}_\sigma = - \frac{u_\sigma^{(d)} V}{2}
\]
Interestingly enough, in principle an anisotropy in the normal diffusion between $x$ and $y$ direction emerges: such a feature is referred to as ``nematicity''. A schematics of anisotropic mixed symmetry hopping is depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:anisotropic-hopping}: as is evident, for each bond linking two sites the hopping parameter can be a positive or negative combination of the two components, whether the link direction is vertical or horizontal. Naturally, the solution needs to be invariant under exchange $x \leftrightarrow y$, making the actual SSB of the class $\mathrm{C}_4 \to \mathrm{C}_2 / \mathbb{Z}_2$. For the normal phase, the physical expectation is that nematicity is suppressed; this does not make this discussion somwhat less relevant for more complex phases, such as the superconducting one, for which it is known nematicity in $2$D planar lattices can arise as a source of superconducting long-range ordering \cite{bohmer2022nematicity}.

The fact that hopping is here anisotropic does not break full translational invariance $\mathrm{T}_1^{(x)} \otimes \mathrm{T}_1^{(y)}$. Imposing a free degenerate Fermi gas ground state in Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-cc-EV} necessarily gives a uniform density indeed, whatever the shape of the band. By Fourier-transforming the site number operator according to the convention of Eq.~\eqref{eq:fourier-convention},
\[
	\hat c_{\mathbf{r}\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{r}\sigma} \stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} e^{i (\mathbf{k}-\mathbf{k}') \cdot\mathbf{r}} \hat c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{k}'\sigma}
\]
which obviously implies
\[
	\ev{\hat n_{i\sigma}} = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\]
A perfectly degenerate Fermi gas is always translational invariant and thus cannot exhibit non-uniform symmetry. The fact that hopping is allowed to be anisotropic does not interfere with that: $\mathrm{C}_4$ SSB is not manifested this way.

Note that for null HFPs,
\[
	u_\sigma^{(s^*)} = 0
	\qquad
	u_\sigma^{(d)} = 0
\]
we have, in vector notation and recalling Tab.~\ref{tab:x-wave-real-factors}:
\[
	\tilde{t}_{\mathbf{r}\mathbf{r}'\sigma} = \frac{t}{2} \left[
		\delta_{\mathbf{r}'=\mathbf{r}+\underline{\mathbf{x}}} + \delta_{\mathbf{r}'=\mathbf{r}-\underline{\mathbf{x}}} + \delta_{\mathbf{r}'=\mathbf{r}+\underline{\mathbf{y}}} + \delta_{\mathbf{r}'=\mathbf{r}-\underline{\mathbf{y}}}
	\right]
\]
The fact that $t/2$ appears here instead of $t$ is not inconsistent, it's just a consequence of the equivalence:
\[
	-t \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right] = -\frac{t}{2} \sum_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right]
\]
The diffusion RMP $\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma}$ is to be interpreted like the right-hand side of the above equation, with the kinetic operator becoming:
\[
	\hat H_{\tilde{t}} \equiv -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right]
\]
To limit the sum to NNs is a matter of the symmetry structure of the function $\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma}$.

We can define the direction-dependent hopping as
\begin{align}
	\tilde{t}^{(x)} &\equiv t - \frac{u^{(s^*)} - u^{(d)}}{2} V \\
	\tilde{t}^{(y)} &\equiv t - \frac{u^{(s^*)} + u^{(d)}}{2} V
\end{align}
Both components are controlled by $V$. Evidently $u^{(s^*)}$ is a measure of the direction-averaged hopping shift with respect to $V$, while $u^{(d)}$ measures the hopping anisotropy,
\[
	u^{(s^*)} = \frac{
		\left(
		t-\tilde{t}^{(x)}
		\right) + \left(
		t-\tilde{t}^{(y)}
		\right)
	}{V}
	\qquad
	u^{(d)} = \frac{\tilde{t}^{(y)} - \tilde{t}^{(x)}}{V}
\]
Theoretical introduction to nematicity is over, and we will get back to this later. To complete the phase overview, we move to the computation of the ground-state free energt density.

\section{Normal free energy density}\label{sec:normal-free-energy}

An important quantity to derive in order to compare different phases is free energy density. The mere existence of a MFT solution to a given phase is not enough to understand if the latter is physically established. To compare energy is instead the correct method to understand what phase is dominant. Within this section we sketch a procedure to extract free energy density we will use identically everywhere. Let
\[
	f_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv \frac{F_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})}}{L_x L_y}
\]
being $F_\mathrm{MFT}$ the total free energy. Consider the complete hamiltonian
\[
	\hat H -\mu \hat N \simeq \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} \tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} \hat c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} - \tilde{\mu} \hat N
\]
where $E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ is the (negative) total contraction energy for the normal phase,
\[
	E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv
	-\left[
		E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})} + E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} +  E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}
	\right]
\]
having included the various contraction energies from Eqns.~\eqref{eq:normal-hartree-U-contraction-energy}, \eqref{eq:normal-hartree-V-contraction-energy} and \eqref{eq:normal-fock-V-contraction-energy}.

Let us now take together all terms. From now on, we will omit spin index $\sigma$, making use of the always present $\mathbb{Z}_2$ spin inversion symmetry discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:normal-phase-theory}. For a free Fermi gas, the free energy density is simply obtained by summing four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item The free particles thermal ground state energy density,
	\[
		e_\mathrm{g}^{(\mathrm{N})} = \frac{2}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}-\tilde{\mu}) f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu} )
	\]
	\item The contraction energy density,
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		e_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} &\equiv \frac{E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})}}{L_x L_y} \\
		&= - n^2 U + V \left[
			2zn^2 - 
			\big|
				u^{(s^*)}
			\big|^2 - \big|
				u^{(d)}
			\big|^2
		\right]
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	\item The entropic contribution to free energy
	\[
		e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv -Ts
	\]
	with $T = 1/k_B \beta$ and $s$ the free Fermi gas entropy density for a single spin sector \cite{grosso2014solid},
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-entropy-density}
		s = -\frac{k_B}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \left[
			\left(
				1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\right) \ln \left(
				1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\right) + f(
				\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\vphantom{A^A}
		\right]
	\end{equation}
	We use the identity:
	\[
		\ln \left(
			1-\frac{1}{e^x+1}
		\right) = x+\ln \left(
			\frac{1}{e^x+1}
		\right) 
	\]
	and, substituting $x=\beta (\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}-\tilde{\mu})$, reduce the above expression to:
	\begin{multline*}
		s = -\frac{k_B}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \Big[ \ln \left(
		1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) + f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \\
		- \beta (\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}-\tilde{\mu}) f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\Big]
	\end{multline*}
	Second and fourth terms cancel out, and multiplying by $-2T$ (taking care of spin degeneracy) we have
	\[
		e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} = \frac{2}{\beta L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \ln \left(
		1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) - \frac{2}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}-\tilde{\mu}) f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
	\]
	\item The chemical potential correction
	\[
		e_n^{(\mathrm{N})} = \mu n
	\]
	Note here that we are using the ``physical'' value of $\mu$, not its RMP counterpart $\tilde{\mu}$. The reason for this is discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}.
\end{enumerate}
Thus, composing everything
\[
\begin{aligned}
	f_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})} &\equiv e_\mathrm{g}^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_n^{(\mathrm{N})} \nonumber \\
	&= \frac{2}{\beta L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \ln \left(
	1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) - V \left[ 
		\big|
			u^{(s^*)}
		\big|^2 + \big|
			u^{(d)}
		\big|^2
	\right] + \left[
		\mu + n(2zV-U)
	\right] n 
	\label{eq:normal-fMFT}
\end{aligned}
\]
When it comes to algorithmic computation of this quantity, it is important to express $\mu$ in terms of its RMP counterpart $\tilde{\mu}$, as discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}. By doing this we are considering all the effects of MFT decomposition of $\hat H$. However, as was predicted, the RMP $\tilde{\mu} = \mu + n(2zV-U)$ presented in Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-mu-shift} appears because of contraction energy corrections, giving finally
\begin{equation}
	f_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})} = \frac{2}{\beta L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \ln \left(
	1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) - V \left[ 
		\big|
			u^{(s^*)}
		\big|^2 + \big|
			u^{(d)}
		\big|^2
	\right] + \tilde{\mu} n 
\end{equation}
a simple enough formula including all effects. Note that, in computational therms, logarithmic divergences at null argument (occurring in the formula above at energies deep in the Fermi sea) can be difficult to handle. It is then useful to implement some algebraic manipulation to overcome this difficulty in numerical computations.

\section{Theoretical constraints on the normal phase HFPs}\label{sec:normal-constraints}

Within this section we discuss some theoretical constraints on the two HFPs identified in this section. These last are useful in later numerical considerations. The discussion is hereby divided in two parts: first we consider the simpler case of unviolated $\mathrm{C}_4$ symmetry, then we move to anisotropic hopping and SSB.

Before starting, it is useful to spend a few words on the spatial structure of a mixed $s^* \otimes d$-wave band. Let in general for notational simplicity
\[
	\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} = \epsilon^{(s^*)} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) + \epsilon^{(d)} (\cos k_x - \cos k_y)
\]
It is the balance between the two components $\epsilon^{(s^*)}$ and $\epsilon^{(d)}$ that gives the main features of the renormalized band. Consider the $s^*$-wave and $d$-wave structure factors already presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:x-wave-reciprocal-factors}, and define the Magnetic Brillouin Zone (MBZ) and Nematic Brillouin Zone (NBZ) as follows:
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\mathrm{MBZ} &\equiv \left\lbrace
	\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}
	\mid 
	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(s^*)} \ge 0
	\right\rbrace \\
	\mathrm{NBZ} &\equiv \left\lbrace
	\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}
	\mid 
	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(d)} \ge 0
	\right\rbrace
\end{aligned}
\]
represented in the top-left panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:normal-nematic-bands}. In the other panels are reported examples of mixed symmetry bands, for positive components. The respective opposite sign counterparts are obtained easily by the following rules:
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\epsilon^{(s^*)} < 0 \qq{and} \epsilon^{(d)} < 0
	\quad&\implies\quad \text{Invert colours in Fig.~\ref{fig:MBZ-sectors}} \\
	\epsilon^{(s^*)} > 0 \qq{and} \epsilon^{(d)} < 0
	\quad&\implies\quad \text{Rotate plots by $\pi/2$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:MBZ-sectors}} \\
	\epsilon^{(s^*)} < 0 \qq{and} \epsilon^{(d)} > 0
	\quad&\implies\quad \text{Rotate plots by $\pi/2$ and invert colours in Fig.~\ref{fig:MBZ-sectors}} \\
\end{aligned}
\]

It is useful to concentrate on the MBZ and the NBZ, being various computations later performed precisely over these domains. Starting from the intersections of the two magnetic zones, both can be factored in two sectors of interest,
\[
	\mathrm{MBZ} = \mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{B}
	\qquad
	\mathrm{NBZ} = \mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{C}
\]
being, accordingly to Fig.~\ref{fig:BZ-sectors},
\[
	\mathcal{A} \equiv \mathcal{A}_1 \cup \mathcal{A}_2
	\qquad
	\mathcal{B} \equiv \mathcal{B}_1 \cup \mathcal{B}_2
	\qquad
	\mathcal{C} \equiv \mathcal{C}_1 \cup \mathcal{C}_2
\]
These domains satisfy:
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\mathcal{A} &\equiv \left\lbrace
	\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}
	\mid 
	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(s^*)} \ge 0 \qq{and} 	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(d)} \ge 0
	\right\rbrace \\
	&= \mathrm{MBZ} \cap \mathrm{NBZ} \\
	\mathcal{B} &\equiv \left\lbrace
	\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}
	\mid 
	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(s^*)} \ge 0 \qq{and} 	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(d)} \le 0
	\right\rbrace \\
	&= \mathrm{MBZ} \setminus \mathrm{NBZ} \\
	\mathcal{C} &\equiv \left\lbrace
	\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}
	\mid 
	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(s^*)} \le 0 \qq{and} 	\varphi_\mathbf{k}^{(d)} \ge 0
	\right\rbrace \\
	&= \mathrm{NBZ} \setminus \mathrm{MBZ}
\end{aligned}
\]
and will be used later on. Importantly, whatever the shape of the mixed band, the following equation always holds
\[
	\abs{k_x} = \frac{\pi}{2} \qq{and} \abs{k_y} = \frac{\pi}{2}
	\quad\implies\quad
	\tilde{\epsilon}_{(k_x,k_y)} = 0
\]
since only cosines are involved. This identifies four node points, represented as black dots in Fig.~\ref{fig:MBZ-sectors}.

\subsection{Constraints in presence of a robust $\mathrm{C}_4$ symmetry and bands shrinking}\label{subsec:normal-bands-shrinking}

Consider the simpler situation where $\mathrm{C}_4$ symmetry is imposed. This implies that the renormalized bands are
\[
	\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} \equiv (u^{(s^*)} V - 2t) (\cos k_x + \cos k_y)
\]
with a single self-consistency equation
\[
	u^{(s^*)} = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\]
Just by looking at these two equations we can conclude:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item For $t>0$, there is no stable solution admitting $u^{(s^*)}\le0$. Let us prove this: suppose instead $u^{(s^*)}\le0$. A generic sum over the BZ is identically written as
	\[
		\sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} g_\mathbf{k} = \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{MBZ}} \left[
		g_\mathbf{k} + g_{\mathbf{k}+\bm{\pi}}
		\right]
	\]
	where we defined the vector $\bm{\pi}\equiv(\pi,\pi)$. The self-consistency equation thus reads
	\begin{align}
		u^{(s^*)} &= \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{MBZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \left[
			f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) + f(\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}+\bm{\pi}};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right] \nonumber \\
		&= \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{MBZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \left[
			f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right] \label{eq:normal-us*-constraint-int1}
	\end{align}
	having we used the antisymmetry of $s^*$-wave functions by $\bm{\pi}$ shifts. Now, if $u^{(s^*)}\le0$ the renormalized band $\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}$ is negative inside the MBZ and positive outside, implying
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathrm{MBZ}
		\quad\colon\quad
		f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ge f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
	\]
	the equality only being satisfied on the MBZ contour. Then, since inside the MBZ it holds by definition $\cos k_x + \cos k_y \ge 0$, the above equations imply
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} \le 0 
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(s^*)} \ge 0
	\]
	To exclude the possibility of $u^{(s^*)}=0$, it suffices to notice that from the argument above such a situation only arises when the MBZ contour degenerates to the entirety of the MBZ itself -- which is, when the band is flat. But that cannot be the case for $u^{(s^*)} = 0$, as long as $t>0$. Thus we have proven that
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} > 0
	\]
	\item For $t>0$, there is no stable solution admitting $u^{(s^*)} \ge 2t/V$. We proceed exactly as did for the previous point, up to the passage:
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{MBZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \left[
		f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right]
	\]
	Now, if $u^{(s^*)} \ge 2t/V$ this means the prefactor in front of the renormalized bands is positive. This implies the renormalized band $\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}$ is positive inside the MBZ and negative outside, implying
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathrm{MBZ}
		\quad\colon\quad
		f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \le f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
	\]
	thus we have
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} \ge 2t/V
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(s^*)} \le 0
	\]
	which is absurd. Thus
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V
	\]
\end{enumerate}
Collecting the two points above, we have the constraint on $u^{(s^*)}$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-us*-constraint}
	\mathrm{C}_4
	\quad\implies\quad
	0 < u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V
\end{equation}
Interestingly enough, the self-consistent procedure predicts a positive convergence value for the simplest HFP, excluding the possibility of nullifying the effective hopping as well as bands sign inversion. We can denominate this net effect ``bands shrinking''.

\subsection{Extension to a $\mathrm{C}_2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry}\label{subsec:normal-bands-shrinking-C2}

Let us extend the arguments above to a weaker symmetry, allowing for
\[
	\mathrm{C}_4 \SSB \mathrm{C}_2/\mathbb{Z}_2
\]
We work with the full, anisotropic band of Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-bands-renormalization}. The proofs of this section are somewhat more involuted, and lead to the conclusion that within the normal phase the $\mathrm{C}_4$ structure must be preserved, leading us back to the derivation of the previous section.

\begin{enumerate}
	\item It is easy to show that a pure $d$-wave band is impossible to realize at any finite $t>0$: if the band is purey $d$-wave, then the relative Fermi-Dirac distribution cannot admit $s^*$-wave components,
	\[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) = 0
	\]
	thus it must be $u^{(s^*)}=0$. We have
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} = 2t/V 
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(s^*)} = 0
	\]
	which is absurd at finite values. A purely $d$-wave band is impossible.
	\item Let for instance
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} > 2t/V > 0
		\qquad
		u^{(d)} > 0
	\]
	The resulting band is analogous to the ones of Fig.~\ref{fig:normal-nematic-bands}. Within this structure and using the notation of Fig.~\ref{subfig:MBZ-sectors}, it always holds
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{A}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} \ge 0
	\]
	Recollecting Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-us*-constraint-int1} which is identically written as
	\begin{multline*}
		u^{(s^*)} = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathcal{A}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \left[
			f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right] \\
		+ \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathcal{B}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \left[
		f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right]
	\end{multline*}
	%

		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{../Project/src/labs/bands/normal-nematic-bands-pp.pdf}
		\caption{Theoretical example of a band with $u^{(s^*)} > 2t/V$ and $u^{(d)}>0$. Two example points are reported, connected by a $\pi/2$ rotation as given in Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-k'=Rk}.}
		\label{fig:normal-nematic-bands-pp}
	\end{figure}
	%
	The sum with $\mathcal{B}$ requires a little care: in fact, as is evident from the bottom-left panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:normal-nematic-bands}, such a region contains nodal curves across which bands change sign.	
	Let $\mathcal{R}$ be the clockwise $\pi/2$ rotation: then, any point in $\mathcal{B}$ can be seen as the rotation of a point in $\mathcal{A}$
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-k'=Rk}
		\forall \mathbf{k}' \in \mathcal{B} 
		\quad\exists\quad \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{A} \mid \mathbf{k}' = \mathcal{R} \mathbf{k}
	\end{equation}
	A graphical rendition of this idea is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:normal-nematic-bands-pp}.
	
	Now: both $\epsilon^{(s^*)}$ and $\epsilon^{(d)}$ are positive by construction, then it holds
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{A}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\abs{\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}} \ge \abs{\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}}}
	\]
	which a trivial consequence of the fact that $\abs{a-b} \le \abs{a+b}$ for positive $a$, $b$ and
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{A}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\begin{aligned}
			\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} &= \overbrace{\epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right)}^{\ge 0} + \overbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }^{\ge 0} \\
			\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}} &= \underbrace{\epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right)}_{\ge 0} - \underbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }_{\ge 0}
		\end{aligned}
	\]
	Now, the Fermi-Dirac distribution is monotonically descendent: this means that
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} \le \tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}}
		\quad&\implies\quad
		f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ge f(\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \\
		-\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}} \le \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} 
		\quad&\implies\quad f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ge 
		f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	thus we have
	\begin{multline*}
		u^{(s^*)} = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathcal{A}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) \\
		\times \left[
			f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) + f(\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(-\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right]
	\end{multline*}
	which leads to an absurd,
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} > 2t/V 
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(s^*)} \le 0
	\]
	\item An identical argument, exchanging the roles of $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, can be carried out to exclude the possibility of
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} > 2t/V > 0
		\qquad
		u^{(d)} < 0
	\]
	thus we can self-consistently claim $u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V$.
	\item Let now
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} < 0
		\qquad
		u^{(d)} > 0
	\]
	Within this structure, it always holds
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} \ge 0
	\]
	Proceed exactly as before, with identical meaning of the notation and inverted roles for $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, as drawn in Fig.~\ref{fig:normal-nematic-bands-mp}.
	%

		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{../Project/src/labs/bands/normal-nematic-bands-mp.pdf}
		\caption{Theoretical example of a band with $u^{(s^*)} < 0$ and $u^{(d)}>0$. Two example points are reported, connected by a $\pi/2$ rotation.}
		\label{fig:normal-nematic-bands-mp}
	\end{figure}
	%
	First, we have
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\abs{\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}} \le \abs{\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}}}
	\]
	which a trivial consequence of the fact that $\abs{a-b} \ge \abs{a+b}$ for $a\le0$ and $b\ge0$, and
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{B}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\begin{aligned}
			\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} &= \overbrace{\epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right)}^{\ge 0} + \overbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }^{\le 0} \\
			\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}} &= \underbrace{\epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right)}_{\ge 0} - \underbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }_{\le 0}
		\end{aligned}
	\]
	By repeating identical steps as before we end up with the implication
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} < 0
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(s^*)} \ge 0
	\]
	which is absurd.
	\item An identical argument, exchanging the roles of $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, can be carried out to exclude the possibility of
	\[
		u^{(s^*)} < 0
		\qquad
		u^{(d)} < 0
	\]
	\newcounter{enumTemp}
	\setcounter{enumTemp}{\theenumi}
\end{enumerate}
From the chain of derivations above, we can conclude self-consistently that also in the nematic structure the constraint of Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-us*-constraint} needs to be fulfilled. In the $s^*$-wave channel, partial breaking of rotational symmetry is not enough to modify the main effect identified under $\mathrm{C}_4$ symmetry, ``bands shrinking''. What is missing is the behaviour of the $d$-wave channel when said effect takes place. Continuing the enumeration above,

\begin{enumerate}
	\setcounter{enumi}{\theenumTemp}
	\item Let $0 < u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V$ and $u^{(d)} > 0$. The self-consistency equation for $u^{(d)}$ reads
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		u^{(d)} &= \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x - \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \\
		&= \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{NBZ}} (\cos k_x - \cos k_y) \left[
			f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f(\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right]
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	where we recognized, as is shown in Fig.~\ref{subfig:NBZ-sectors}, that $\mathrm{BZ} \setminus \mathrm{NBZ} = \mathcal{R} \mathrm{NBZ}$. It holds:
	\[
		\forall \mathbf{k} \in \mathrm{NBZ}
		\quad\colon\quad
		\begin{aligned}
			\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} &= \epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right) + \overbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }^{\ge 0} \\
			\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}} &= \epsilon^{(s^*)} \left( \cos k_x + \cos k_y \right) - \underbrace{\epsilon^{(d)}  \left( \cos k_x - \cos k_y \right) }_{\ge 0}
		\end{aligned}
	\]
	thus $\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathcal{R}\mathbf{k}} < \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}$, and inserting this fact in the self-consistency equation above we get
	\[
		0 < u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V
		\qq{and}
		u^{(d)} > 0
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(d)} < 0
	\]
	which, as always, is absurd.
	\item An identical argument, with inverted inequalities, can be carried out to exclude the possibility of
	\[
		0 < u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V
		\qq{and}
		u^{(d)} < 0
		\quad\implies\quad
		u^{(d)} > 0
	\]
\end{enumerate}
These last two facts were the missing informations. Importantly, self-consistency excludes the possibility of a $\mathrm{C}_4 \to \mathrm{C}_2$ SSB in the normal phase, giving the constraint
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-us*-ud-constraint}
	0 < u^{(s^*)} < 2t/V
	\qq{and}
	u^{(d)} = 0
\end{equation}
A consequence of this is that there is no need at all to bother using $u^{(d)}$ as an HFP. This is physically expected but non trivial. 

At this point, bands shrinking is a guaranteed effect, and the point to be asked is where the converged value of $u^{(s^*)}$ will be positioned in its admissibility range, based on the model parameter. This question is the subject of next sections.

\subsection{Expected dependence on $\delta$}

Let us start this analysis in the low-temperature limit, namely $\beta \gg 1$. Consider the self-consistency equation of Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-ehm-ugamma-self-consistencies} seen as a functional of temperature and chemical potential,
\[
	u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\]
in the zero-temperature limit, as long as the shrunk band is not flat, it holds
\[
	\lim_{\beta \to \infty} u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\epsilon_\mathbf{k} \le \tilde{\mu}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y)
\]
Now, at low temperature the Fermi-Dirac slope becomes less steep, a fact that makes the above equation only approximately verified as long as the convergence value of $u^{(s^*)}$ results in a ``not-so-strong'' bands shrinking.

Let us assume this, which means we assume it holds
\[
	u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] \big|_{\beta \gg 1} \simeq \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\epsilon_\mathbf{k} \le \tilde{\mu}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y)
\]
which, at this point, will be true only in an unspecified region of parameters space. Of course, the ``not-so-strong'' shrinking assumption implies the fact that a FS is well-defined, being the band non-flat. While at half filling, because of PHS,
\[
	\delta = 0
	\quad\implies\quad
	\mathrm{FS} = \partial \mathrm{MBZ}
\]
at $\delta>0$ the FS expands over the MBZ boundary, where the form factor $\cos k_x + \cos k_y$ becomes negative. This gives the self-consistency equation
\[
	u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] \big|_{\beta \gg 1} \simeq \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\epsilon_\mathbf{k} \le 0} \abs{\cos k_x + \cos k_y} - \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{0<\epsilon_\mathbf{k} \le \tilde{\mu}} \abs{\cos k_x + \cos k_y}
\]
which, being the sum of a positive and a negative term, is maximized whenever $\tilde{\mu}=0$.

This holds as long as the effective hopping is non-zero, a condition implying together
\[
	\delta_1 > \delta_2
	\quad\implies\quad
	\text{$\mathrm{FS}_1$ encircles $\mathrm{FS}_2$} 
\]
with obvious meaning of the notation. Using this property we conclude
\[
	\tilde{t} > 0
	\qq{and}
	\delta_1 > \delta_2
	\quad\implies\quad
	\lim_{\beta \to \infty} u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] \Big|_{\delta_1} < 
	\lim_{\beta \to \infty} u^{(s^*)}[\beta,\tilde{\mu}] \Big|_{\delta_2}
\]
This is the first expected behaviour of the self-consistent functional $u^{(s^*)}$: if there exists a region of parametric space where the shrinking effect is ``not-so-strong'' to rise the doping level must lower $u^{(s^*)}$. Such a region must exists: at least all the segment $(V=0,\delta)$ is included, since on said region of parametric space the model is just the common free electrons model.
