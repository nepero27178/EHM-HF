\chapter{The normal phase}\label{chap:normal-phase}

This chapter is devoted to the MFT analysis of the ``normal phase'', which is, the one where interacting electrons do not develop an antiferromagnetic or superconducting gap, although undergoing an effective non-trivial transformation. What will be derived here, in particular the hopping renormalization effect, constitutes a peculiarity of effective MFT treatment of the EHM and will be the groundwork of following analyses. Note that this chapter is structured in such a way that what is derived here mathematically is recovered and expanded to more complex phases in next chapters.

\section{The ``normal'' phase and its symmetries}\label{sec:normal-phase-theory}

The normal phase is simply the one where no symmetry is broken, and the many-body ground state preserves the entire $\mathrm{U}(2)$ structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ehm-global-u2-symmetry-u1-su2} as well as translational symmetry. Thus, the normal phase is essentially given by the null gap limit of both the antiferromagnetic and superconducting phases. By discussing it separately now, we highlight a MFT feature of the EHM of particular interest, namely, the insurgence of a new, self-consistent hopping parameter. From Tabs.~\ref{tab:U-wick-terms-phases} and \ref{tab:V-wick-terms-phases}, we know that the relevant Wick's contractions (RWCs) for this phase are
\[
\begin{aligned}
	&\hat H_U 
	&&\text{Hartree contraction} \\
	&\hat H_V \text{ (o.s. sector)}
	&&\text{Hartree contraction} \\
	&\hat H_V \text{ (s.s. sector)}
	&&\text{Hartree and Fock contractions} \\
\end{aligned}	
\]
and since the phase we are establishing obeys translational symmetry, we also know Hartree terms are essentially chemical potential shifts. Next section derives this effect in detail.

In the normal phase, the state retains its full $\mathrm{SU}(2) \times \mathrm{U}^\mathrm{c}(1) \times (\mathrm{Lattice})$ symmetry discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:ehm-symmetries}. Spin is a pure physical degeneracy, being the $\uparrow$ and $\downarrow$ sectors separated, and $\hat H$ is $\mathbb{Z}_2$ invariant with respect to spin inversion, $\sigma \leftrightarrow \overline{\sigma}$. This implies that all physical quantities, as is for the normal phase, should not depend on the spin index. As for the normal phase, we will perform all computations leaving $\sigma$ explicit and using this consideration at the end.

\section{Hartree effects: chemical potential renormalization}\label{sec:normal-hartree-mu}

The first RMP we discuss is $\mu$, whose numerical value is renormalized by Hartree channel interactions within MFT. As is discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}, in the context of numerical simulations at fixed density, the chemical potential is determined self-consistently; thus any net effect that shifts $\mu$ is effectively ignored within (and limitedly to) the iterative algorithm. Renormalization effects become relevant when computing free energy.

\subsection{Hartree channel in the normal phase: analytical derivation}\label{subsec:normal-hartree-analytical}

Within this section we discuss analytically the effects of MFT on the Hartree channel, separating the local and non-local interactions and obtaining a set of HFPs needed to setup an interative HF algorithm.

\paragraph{Local repulsion.}

In the normal phase, the local repulsion $\hat H_U$ decomposes in Hartree channel as
\[
	\hat H_U \simeq
	U \sum_{i} \left[
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\uparrow}
		\rangle \hat n_{i\downarrow} + \hat n_{i\uparrow} \langle 
			\hat n_{i\downarrow}
		\rangle - \langle 
			\hat n_{i\downarrow}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{i\uparrow}
			\rangle
	\right]
\]
For a translationally invariant phase such as the normal phase, it holds
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-density-ansatz}
	\langle \hat n_{i\sigma} \rangle = n
\end{equation}
which gives immediately
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\hat H_U &\simeq
	nU \sum_i \left[
		 \hat n_{i\uparrow} + \hat n_{i\downarrow}
	\right]
	- U \sum_i n^2 \\
	&= nU \times \hat N - E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})}
\end{aligned}
\]
being $\hat N$ the total number operator and $ E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ the ``contraction energy'' (also known as double counting term) due to the Hartree (H) contraction of the $U$ repulsion. The chemical potential is corrected by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:local-hartree-mu-shift}
	\mu \to \mu - nU
\end{equation}
The non-local attraction further corrects $\mu$.

\paragraph{Non-local attraction.}

Let us break down $\hat H_V$ isolating its Hartree RWCs in both o.s. and s.s. sectors:
\begin{multline}
	\hat H_V \simeq 
	\overbrace{
		-V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
			\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \hat n_{j\sigma} + \hat n_{i\sigma} \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
			\rangle - \langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
			\rangle
		\right]
	}^{\mathrm{s.s.}} \\
	\underbrace{
		-V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
			\langle
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}} + \hat n_{i\sigma} \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
			\rangle
			- \langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
			\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
			\rangle
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{o.s.}} \, +\, 
	(\text{all the rest})
	\label{eq:normal-V-wick-decomposition}
\end{multline}
where ``all the rest'' collects all non-Hartree RWCs. Recalling the Ansatz of Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-density-ansatz}, we get
\[
	\hat H_V \simeq  \underbrace{
		-nV \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat n_{j\sigma} + \hat n_{i\sigma}
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{s.s.}}
	\underbrace{
		-nV \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}} + \hat n_{i\sigma}
		\right]
	}_{\mathrm{o.s.}} 
	- E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}
	+ (\text{all the rest})
\]
with $E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ the normal state shift to energy brought by $\hat H_V$. Now, evidently both sums above are just a number operator,
\[
	\hat H_V \simeq -2nzV \times \hat N - E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} + 
	(\text{all the rest})
\]
This accounts for the final Hartree shift of $\mu$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:non-local-hartree-mu-shift}
	\mu \to \mu + 2znV
\end{equation}

Considering the net shift to $\mu$ due to both interactions by combining Eqns.~\eqref{eq:local-hartree-mu-shift}, \eqref{eq:non-local-hartree-mu-shift}, we get
\begin{equation}\label{eq:total-hartree-mu-shift}
	\tilde{\mu} \equiv \mu + n(2zV-U)
\end{equation}
This result remains valid throughout all phases discussed in this text: for the AF phase, the SDW character leaves this shift untouched, while the superconducting phase we are discussion is inherently translational invariant.

\subsection{Hartree contraction energy}\label{subsec:normal-hartree-energy}

The double counting terms accounting for energy shifts in the Hartree channel are, as anticipated:
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= \sum_i \langle 
		\hat n_{i\downarrow}
	\rangle \langle 
		\hat n_{i\uparrow}
	\rangle \\
	&= L_x L_y \times n^2U \label{eq:normal-hartree-U-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
for the local repulsion and 
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\sigma}
		\rangle
		+
		\langle 
			\hat n_{i\sigma}
		\rangle \langle 
			\hat n_{j\overline{\sigma}}
		\rangle
	\right]\\
	&= 2V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma n^2 \\
	&= L_x L_y \times 2zn^2 V \label{eq:normal-hartree-V-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
for the non-local attraction, being $z=4$ the coordination number for the $2$D square lattice. In the last passage, we used that
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\sum_{\ev{ij}} (1) &= \sum_{i\in\mathcal{S}/2} \sum_{j\in\mathrm{NN}(i)} (1) \\
	&= \frac{L_x L_y}{2} \times z
\end{aligned}
\]
as well as spin degeneracy. Note that, correctly, the contraction energy is an extensive quantity. This implies that we can define a contraction energy per site -- something we will do later when computing total free energy density.

\section{Fock effects: bare bands renormalization}\label{sec:normal-fock-bands-renormalization}

Up to now, no relevant changes have been made to the starting situation of the conventional degenerate Fermi gas. The scenario changes now, with the analysis of the Fock RWCs. Even if no gap opens, in the normal phase the particles interact by the means of a non-local attraction, a feature that effectively redefines the bare fermionic bands.

From Wick's decomposition of $\hat H_V$, the only allowed Fock term comes from the same-spin part due to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ symmetry selection rules. Let us focus only on this term when decomposing $\hat H$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:real-space-non-local-interaction-fock}
	\hat H_V \simeq 
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] + (\text{all the rest})
\end{equation}
(note the $+$ sign in front of the displayed term). A bond-wise hopping amplitude can be defined,
\[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \equiv t - V \langle
	\hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{i\sigma}
	\rangle
\]
The effective diffusive hamiltonian is given by
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\hat H_{\tilde{t}} &= \hat H_t + V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + \hc
	\right] \\
	&= - \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma 
	\left[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right]
\end{aligned}
\]
To proceed, it is useful to move to reciprocal space.

\subsection{Fock channel in the normal phase: analytical derivation}\label{subsec:normal-fock-analytical}

In reciprocal space, the effective hopping must be transformed as well. Consider the Fourier Transform $\mathcal{F}$ given in Eq.~\eqref{eq:reciprocal-space-non-local-interaction-explicit}, applied to the displayed part of Eq.~\eqref{eq:real-space-non-local-interaction-fock},
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\langle
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
		\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
		\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\cos \left(
	\delta k_x
	\right)	+ \cos \left(
	\delta k_y
	\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}' \sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int1}
\end{multline}
Here, the $2$ prefactor comes from recognizing that the first two operators in square brackets in the first line generate identical contributions to the full sum; the double counting energy shift is given by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reciprocal-space-non-local-interaction-fock-double-couting}
	E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv \frac{V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}, \mathbf{k}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta k_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta k_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}' \sigma}
	\rangle \langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}'\sigma} 
	\rangle
\end{equation}

Up to this point, this derivation is general and will be re-used in next chapters. Here the specificity of the physical phase settles in: if we assume the normal state to be a free degenerate Fermi gas with renormalized bands, we get
\[
	\langle \hat c_{\mathbf{k}_1\sigma_1} \hat c_{\mathbf{k}_2 \sigma_2} \rangle = \delta_{\mathbf{k}_1=\mathbf{k}_2} \delta_{\sigma_1=\sigma_2} f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\]
being $f$ the Fermi-Dirac distribution,
\[
	f(\epsilon;\beta,\mu) \equiv \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon-\mu)}+1}
\]
It follows that Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int1} becomes
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{K}, \mathbf{k}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\cos \left(
	2 k_x
	\right)	+ \cos \left(
	2 k_y
	\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger 
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k} \sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int2}
\end{multline}
since the only contribution comes from $\mathbf{k}'=-\mathbf{k}$. Let now $\mathbf{q}\equiv\mathbf{K}+\mathbf{k}$, $\mathbf{q}'\equiv\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{k}$. Being for $\ell=x,y$
\[
\begin{aligned}
	\delta q_\ell &\equiv q_\ell - q_\ell' \\
	&= (K_\ell+k_\ell) - (K_\ell-k_\ell) \\
	&= 2k_\ell
\end{aligned}
\]
we reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int1} to
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \frac{2V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{q}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta q_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta q_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger 
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int3}
\end{multline}
Recall now the result of Eq.~\eqref{eq:coscos-symmetries-decomposition},
\[
	\cos \left( \delta q_x \right)	+ \cos \left( \delta q_y  \right) = \frac 1 2 \sum_\gamma \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)} \varphi_{\mathbf{q}'}^{(\gamma)*}
	\qq{for $\gamma \in \lbrace s^*, p_x, p_y, d_{x^2-y^2} \rbrace$}
\]
Thanks to this handy property, we reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int3} to
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} \sum_\gamma \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_\mathbf{q\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)}
		u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\right] \times V
	\sum_{\mathbf{q}'\in\mathrm{BZ}}
	\varphi_{\mathbf{q}'}^{(\gamma)*}
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int4}
\end{multline}
where we defined the EV:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-uqsigma-def}
	u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} \equiv \langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
\end{equation}
Of course, the numerical value of $u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}$ is given by the Fermi-Dirac distribution; however, for the sake of reproducibility of this derivation in next chapters, we omit that for a moment. Let $u^{(\gamma)}_\sigma$ be its $\gamma$-wave component,
\[
	u^{(\gamma)}_\sigma = \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)}
	\langle
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\rangle
\]

Now, the bare bands $\epsilon_\mathbf{k}$ are $s^*$-wave symmetric, but in general $\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}$ can exhibit a more complex symmetry. Surely, due to spatial inversion invariance, it must be
\[
	u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} = u_{-\mathbf{q}\sigma}
\]
thus
\[
	u_\sigma^{(p_\ell)} = 0
	\qq{for}
	\ell\in\lbrace x,y \rbrace
\]
recalling the antisymmetric form factors of Tab~\ref{tab:x-wave-reciprocal-factors}. However, $u_\sigma^{(s^*)}$ and $u_\sigma^{(d)}$ are in general non-zero. Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-V-fock-int4} becomes
\begin{multline}
	V \sum_{\ev{ij}} \sum_\sigma \left[
	\langle
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} + 
	\hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle - \langle \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma}
	\rangle \langle \hat c_{j\sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{i\sigma} \rangle
	\right] \\
	\stackrel{\mathcal{F}}{=} V
	\sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \sum_\sigma
	\left[
		u^{(s^*)}_\sigma
		\varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(s^*)*} + u^{(d)}_\sigma
		\varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(d)*}
	\right]
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}^\dagger
	\hat c_{\mathbf{q}\sigma} - E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} \label{eq:normal-V-fock-int5}
\end{multline}
Let
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normal-bands-renormalization}
	\tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} \equiv \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + u^{(s^*)}_\sigma V (\cos q_x + \cos q_y) + u^{(d)}_\sigma V (\cos q_x - \cos q_y)
\end{equation}
This equation defines the bands renormalization. The fact that both a $s^*$ and a $d$-wave component appear introduces an interesting anisotropy that is discusses in next section. Tab.~\ref{tab:normal-RWCs} lists the various RWCs and their net effect on the MFT hamiltonian.

\subsection{Fock contraction energy}\label{subsec:normal-fock-energy}

Finally, let us derive the Fock contraction energy by expanding Eq.~\eqref{eq:reciprocal-space-non-local-interaction-fock-double-couting}:
\begin{align}
	E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} &= \frac{V}{L_x L_y} \sum_{ \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{q}'} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\cos \left(
			\delta q_x
		\right)	+ \cos \left(
			\delta q_y
		\right)	
	\right]	
	\langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q} \sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{q}' \sigma}
	\rangle \langle
		\hat c_{\mathbf{q} \sigma}^\dagger  \hat c_{\mathbf{q}' \sigma}
	\rangle \nonumber \\
	&= L_x L_y \times V \sum_{\gamma,\sigma} \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}^{(\gamma)} u_{\mathbf{q}\sigma}
	\right] \left[
		\frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{q}'\in\mathrm{BZ}} \varphi_\mathbf{q}'^{(\gamma)*} u_{\mathbf{q}'\sigma}
	\right] \nonumber \\
	&= L_x L_y \times V \sum_\sigma \left[
		\big|
			u_\sigma^{(s^*)}
		\big|^2 + \big|
			u_\sigma^{(d)}
		\big|^2
	\right] \label{eq:normal-fock-V-contraction-energy}
\end{align}
This expression gives the Fock contraction energy coming from the $\hat H_V$ operator. The particularly relevant result, here, is the fact that the full spectral amplitude of $u_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}$ here is weighted and contributes to contraction energy with a $V$ linear prefactor. This suggests that a mixed-symmetry band could be energetically preferred.

\subsection{Extending hopping to $d$-wave bands}\label{subsec:normal-d-wave-hopping}

\begin{figure}

From Tabs.~\ref{tab:x-wave-real-factors} and \ref{tab:x-wave-reciprocal-factors} we can inverse Fourier transform the form factors to get the full effective hopping,
\[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} = \sum_{\gamma\in\lbrace s^*,s \rbrace} t^{(\gamma)}_\sigma \varphi_{ij}^{(\gamma)}
\]
with
\[
	t^{(s^*)}_\sigma = t - \frac{u_\sigma^{(s^*)} V}{2}
	\qquad
	t^{(d)}_\sigma = - \frac{u_\sigma^{(d)} V}{2}
\]
Interestingly enough, in principle an anisotropy in the normal diffusion between $x$ and $y$ direction emerges. A schematics of anisotropic mixed symmetry hopping is depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:anisotropic-hopping}: as is evident, for each bond linking two sites the hopping parameter can be a positive or negative combination of the two components whether the link direction is vertical or horizontal. \todo

Note that for null HFPs,
\[
	u_\sigma^{(s^*)} = 0

	u_\sigma^{(d)} = 0
\]
we have:
\[
	\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} = \frac{t}{2} \left[
		\delta_{j=i+\delta_x} + \delta_{j=i-\delta_x} + \delta_{j=i+\delta_y} + \delta_{j=i-\delta_y}
	\right]
\]
The fact that $t/2$ appears here instead of $t$ is not inconsistent, it's just a consequence of the equivalence:

	\right] = -\frac{t}{2} \sum_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}} \sum_\sigma \left[

	\right]
\]
The diffusion RMP $\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma}$ is to be interpreted like the right-hand side of the above equation, with the kinetic operator becoming:
\[
	\hat H_{\tilde{t}} \equiv -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}} \sum_\sigma \left[
		\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma} \hat c_{i\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{j\sigma} + \hc
	\right]
\]
To limit the sum to NNs is a matter of the symmetry structure of the function $\tilde{t}_{ij\sigma}$.

\section{Normal free energy density}\label{sec:normal-free-energy}

An important quantity to derive in order to compare different phases is free energy density, defined as
\[
	f_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv \frac{F_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})}}{L_x L_y}
\]
being $F_\mathrm{MFT}$ the total free energy. Consider the complete hamiltonian
\[
	\hat H \simeq \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} \tilde{\epsilon}_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} \hat c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}^\dagger \hat c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} - \tilde{\mu} \hat N
\]
where $E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})}$ is the (negative) total contraction energy for the normal phase,
\[
	E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv
	-\left[
		E_{\mathrm{H}/U}^{(\mathrm{N})} + E_{\mathrm{H}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})} +  E_{\mathrm{F}/V}^{(\mathrm{N})}
	\right]
\]
having included the various contraction energies from Eqns.~\eqref{eq:normal-hartree-U-contraction-energy}, \eqref{eq:normal-hartree-V-contraction-energy} and \eqref{eq:normal-fock-V-contraction-energy}.

Let us now take together all terms. From now on, we will omit spin index $\sigma$, making use of the always present $\mathbb{Z}_2$ spin inversion symmetry discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:normal-phase-theory}. For a free Fermi gas, the free energy density is simply obtained by summing four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item The free particles thermal ground state energy density,
	\[
		e_\mathrm{g}^{(\mathrm{N})} = \frac{2}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu} )
	\]
	\item The contraction energy density,
	\[
	\begin{aligned}
		e_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} &\equiv \frac{E_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})}}{L_x L_y} \\
		&= - n^2 U - 2V \left[
			zn^2 + 
			\big|
				u^{(s^*)}
			\big|^2 + \big|
				u^{(d)}
			\big|^2
		\right]
	\end{aligned}
	\]
	\item The entropic free energy
	\[
		e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} \equiv -Ts
	\]
	with $T = 1/k_B \beta$ and $s$ the free Fermi gas entropy density \cite{grosso2014solid},
	\[
		s = -\frac{2 k_B}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \left[
			\left(
				1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\right) \ln \left(
				1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\right) + f(
				\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
			\ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\right]
	\]
	We use the identity:
	\[
		\ln \left(
			1-\frac{1}{e^x+1}
		\right) = x+\ln \left(
			\frac{1}{e^x+1}
		\right) 
	\]
	and reduce the above expression to:
	\begin{multline*}
		s = -\frac{2 k_B}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \Big[ \ln \left(
		1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) + f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \\
		- \beta \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) - f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \ln f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
		\Big]
	\end{multline*}
	Second and fourth terms cancel out, and we have
	\[
		e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} = \frac{2}{\beta L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \ln \left(
		1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) - \frac{2}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k} f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu})
	\]
	\item The chemical potential correction
	\[
		e_n^{(\mathrm{N})} = \mu n
	\]
	Note here that we are using the ``physical'' value of $\mu$, not its RMP counterpart $\tilde{\mu}$. The reason for this is discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}.
\end{enumerate}
Thus, composing everything
\begin{align}
	f_\mathrm{MFT}^{(\mathrm{N})} &\equiv e_\mathrm{g}^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_\mathrm{c}^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_s^{(\mathrm{N})} + e_n^{(\mathrm{N})} \nonumber \\
	&= \frac{2}{\beta L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} \ln \left(
	1- f( \tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k}; \beta,\tilde{\mu}) \right) - n^2 U - 2V \left[
		zn^2 + 
		\big|
			u^{(s^*)}
		\big|^2 + \big|
			u^{(d)}
		\big|^2
	\right] + \mu n \label{eq:normal-fMFT}
\end{align}
When it comes to algorithmic computation of this quantity, it is important to express $\mu$ in terms of its RMP counterpart $\tilde{\mu}$, as discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:fixed-density-role-mu}. By doing this we are considering all the effects of MFT decomposition of $\hat H$.

\section{HF iterative algorithm sketch}

We are finally able to move to the computational part of this chapter. The aim is to write down an algorithm of the kind of Sec.~\ref{subsec:hf-sketch}. For the normal phase, the HFPs vector has two components:
\[
	\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
		u^{(s^*)} \\ u^{(d)}
	\end{bmatrix}
\]
As before, we treat spin as a pure degeneracy. The HFPs are characterized by the self-consistency equations
\begin{align}
	u^{(s^*)} &\equiv \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x + \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu}) \\
	u^{(d)} &\equiv \frac{1}{L_x L_y} \sum_{\mathbf{k}\in\mathrm{BZ}} (\cos k_x - \cos k_y) f(\tilde{\epsilon}_\mathbf{k};\beta,\tilde{\mu})
\end{align}
The resulting model has two RMPs:
\begin{align}
	\tilde{t}_{ij} &\equiv \left(
		t - \frac{u^{(s^*)}V}{2}
	\right) \varphi_{ij}^{(s^*)} - \frac{u^{(d)}V}{2} \varphi_{ij}^{(d)} \\
	\tilde{\mu} &\equiv \mu + n (2zV-U)
\end{align}
the second one being only dependent on the model parameters and not on HFPs. We can define the direction-dependent hopping as
\begin{align}
	\tilde{t}^{(x)} &\equiv t - \frac{u^{(s^*)} - u^{(d)}}{2} V \\
	\tilde{t}^{(y)} &\equiv t - \frac{u^{(s^*)} + u^{(d)}}{2} V
\end{align}
Both components are controlled by $V$. Evidently $u^{(s^*)}$ is a measure of the direction-averaged hopping shift with respect to $V$, while $u^{(d)}$ measures the hopping anisotropy with respect to $V$. Finally, the phase free energy is given by Eq.~\eqref{eq:normal-fMFT}. \todo
